## Start With Your Profile, But Don't Get Stuck There

Your profile matters. Obviously. But I've seen people spend weeks tweaking their banner image while never actually posting anything, and that's backwards.

Fix the essentials first.

**Your headline** should tell people what you do and who you help, not just your job title. "Director at XYZ Group" tells nobody anything useful. "Helping Sydney homeowners refinance smarter | Mortgage Broker" is a completely different thing.

**Your About section** should sound like you talking, not a third-person bio written for an annual report. Write it in first person. Be specific about what you do, who you help, and what makes you different. Throw in something a little personal if it feels right.

**Your featured section** is prime real estate. Use it. Pin your best post, a testimonial, a lead magnet, something that makes a visitor want to stick around.

Profile sorted. Now the real work begins.

## What to Actually Post for LinkedIn Personal Branding

This is where most people freeze. They don't know what to say, so they either post nothing, or they share someone else's article with zero commentary and call it a day.

Neither works.

The content that actually builds a personal brand tends to fall into a few buckets:

- **Things you've learned from experience**, not theory. The stuff that only comes from having done the work.
- **Opinions on your industry**, even if they're a bit spicy. Agreeable content gets scrolled past. Considered opinions get engagement.
- **Behind-the-scenes moments**, the messy middle of running a business or building a career. People connect with process, not just polished outcomes.
- **Client stories and case studies**, told with permission and with enough detail to be genuinely interesting.

You don't need to post every day. Honestly, three times a week is plenty if the content is good. Consistency matters more than volume. LinkedIn growth Australia comes from showing up regularly over months, not from one viral post.

That's the stuff that gets people sliding into your DMs saying "I've been following you for ages, I'd love to have a chat."

LinkedIn content Australia that performs well has a recognisable voice behind it. Readers should be able to cover the name on a post and still know it's yours. That takes time to develop, but it starts with writing like you talk, not like you think you should sound.

## How Often Should You Post, Really?

Look, there's no magic number. But here's what I'd suggest as a starting point.

Three posts per week is a solid cadence for most professionals. That's enough to stay visible in the feed, build a content library over time, and keep the algorithm relatively happy, without burning yourself out or running out of things to say.

Pair that with genuine engagement, commenting thoughtfully on other people's posts, responding to every comment on your own, and you've got a LinkedIn content strategy Australia that actually works.

One thing worth noting: engagement drops off sharply when you disappear. Even two or three weeks of silence can set you back. So if you know you won't be consistent, sort out a system before you start. That might mean batching content on Sunday afternoons, or it might mean exploring done for you LinkedIn content so the posting never falls over, even when life gets busy.
