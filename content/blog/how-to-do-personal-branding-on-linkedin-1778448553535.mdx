Most LinkedIn profiles read like a corporate memo nobody asked for.

Job titles stacked on job titles. A summary that says "results-driven professional with a passion for excellence." A profile photo from a work Christmas party in 2019.

That's not personal branding. That's just... existing on the internet.

If you actually want to know how to do personal branding on LinkedIn, the honest answer is that it starts way before you touch the keyboard. It starts with figuring out what you actually want to be known for.

## First, Get Clear on Your "Thing"

Personal branding on LinkedIn is not about pretending to be someone you're not. It's about being very deliberately, consistently yourself, in a way that the right people notice.

Ask yourself: if someone looked at your last ten posts, your headline, and your about section, what would they think you do? Who would they think you help? Would they even remember you?

Most people's answer is no.

Pick a lane. Not forever, not a cage, just a direction. Are you the finance person who makes tax simple for small business owners? The project manager who talks about leadership in plain English? The sales leader who's brutally honest about what actually closes deals?

Specificity is what makes people follow you. Vagueness is what makes them scroll past.

## Your LinkedIn Profile Is a Landing Page, Not a Resume

This is the thing most people get wrong. Dead wrong, honestly.

Your LinkedIn profile is not a list of everywhere you've worked. It's a pitch to anyone who lands on it, whether that's a potential client, a recruiter, a speaking organiser, or a journalist looking for a source.

Treat it like one.

**Headline:** Don't just put your job title. Write something that says what you do and who you help. "Helping Australian startups raise their first round" is infinitely more interesting than "Founder at XYZ Capital."

**Banner image:** Use this space. A custom banner with your name, what you do, and maybe a website takes about twenty minutes in Canva and makes your profile look a hundred times more intentional.

**About section:** Write in first person. Tell a story. Why do you do what you do? What's the specific problem you solve? End with a call to action, even a simple one like "feel free to connect if you're working through X."

**Featured section:** Pin your best content here. A post that performed well, a case study, a link to your website. Don't leave this blank.

## Posting Consistently Is Where Most People Fall Apart

Here's the uncomfortable truth about LinkedIn personal branding: profiles are just the foundation. Content is the engine.

You cannot build a recognisable personal brand by updating your profile once a year and occasionally liking other people's posts. You have to show up.

That does not mean posting every single day or turning your life into a LinkedIn documentary. Three times a week is plenty. More important than frequency is consistency and quality.

A few content types that genuinely work for personal branding on LinkedIn:

- **Lessons from real experience.** Not "here are five productivity tips." More like "I spent three years managing remote teams and the one thing nobody tells you is this."
- **Opinions.** Controversial enough to spark conversation, not so outrageous you embarrass your clients.
- **Behind the scenes.** The messy middle of a project, a decision you made and why, a mistake and what it taught you.
- **Simple frameworks.** If you explain how you think about a problem in a clear, repeatable way, people will remember you for it.

Short posts work. Long posts work. What doesn't work is generic, nobody-could-object-to-this content that says absolutely nothing.

## How to Actually Sound Like Yourself

This is where personal branding on LinkedIn gets hard for a lot of professionals.

Writing about yourself online feels uncomfortable. You don't want to brag. You don't want to seem like you're performing. You're not sure what's worth saying.

Look, everyone starts there. Everyone.

A few things that help: write like you're sending a message to one specific person, not broadcasting to an auditorium. Read your post out loud before you hit publish. If you'd never say a sentence in real life, cut it.

Ditch the jargon. "Synergising cross-functional stakeholder outcomes" is not how you talk at the pub, so it's not how you should talk on LinkedIn either.

## Building a Network That Actually Does Something

Personal branding on LinkedIn is not a solo sport.

Commenting thoughtfully on other people's posts, especially people in your industry or your target audience, is one of the fastest ways to get visibility. Not "great post!" comments. Actual thoughts, questions, pushback, additions.

Send connection requests with a message. Thirty words explaining why you're connecting is worth more than ten thousand cold follows with no context.

Engage with your own comments too. When someone replies to your post, reply back. The algorithm loves it. More importantly, people notice when you actually show up in your own conversations.

## The Long Game (And Why It's Worth Playing)

Personal branding on LinkedIn does not happen in a week. Or a month, honestly.

The professionals who build genuine authority on the platform, the ones clients seek out, the ones who get approached for speaking gigs and board seats and media opportunities, they've been at it for a year or two or five. Quietly, consistently, showing up.

The good news is that most people quit early. Which means staying in the game is, on its own, a kind of competitive advantage.

Start with your profile. Get that right. Then commit to posting three times a week for ninety days and actually see what happens.

You might be surprised.
