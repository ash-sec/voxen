# How to Grow on LinkedIn in 2026 (What's Actually Working Right Now)

Nobody told you LinkedIn would feel like a part-time job, did they.

And yet here you are, staring at a blank post box wondering why your last three updates got twelve impressions and a pity-like from your mum's colleague.

Growing on LinkedIn in 2026 is genuinely different from what it was even eighteen months ago. The algorithm has shifted, user behaviour has changed, and the old playbook of "post motivational quotes and hustle harder" is well and truly dead.

So let's get into what's actually moving the needle.

---

## Your Profile Is Either Doing Work or Losing You Leads

Before you write a single post, your profile needs to be sorted. Honestly, this is where most people fall over.

Your headline is the big one. It's not your job title. It's a one-line answer to "why should I care about you?" If your headline currently reads "Senior Manager at [Company]," you're invisible to anyone who doesn't already know you.

Think about who you're trying to reach and what problem you solve for them. That's your headline.

Your About section is the same deal. Write it in first person, keep it conversational, and get specific fast. You've got maybe six seconds before someone scrolls away.

Banner image, featured section, a recent post pinned at the top. Small things, but they signal that you're actually active and worth following.

### The "Creator Mode" Question

Turn it on. Simple. It shifts your profile from connection-focused to follower-focused, which matters enormously if you're trying to build an audience beyond your immediate network.

---

## How to Grow on LinkedIn in 2026: Content That Actually Gets Seen

Right, the meaty bit.

LinkedIn's algorithm in 2026 is heavily rewarding what it calls "meaningful engagement." That basically means comments, saves and shares are weighted way higher than likes. A post with forty comments beats one with four hundred likes, every time.

What drives comments? Honestly, it's posts that make people feel something or think about something differently. Controversy works, but you don't have to be provocative for the sake of it. Disagreeing with a common industry assumption, sharing a mistake you made, asking a genuinely interesting question, these all get people talking.

Short-form text posts are still outperforming most formats for organic reach. Not always, but often enough that you shouldn't ignore them.

Video has had a bit of a resurgence too. Vertical video especially, LinkedIn is clearly chasing that short-form audience. But don't stress if video isn't your thing. Plenty of people are growing big audiences with nothing but text.

### Posting Frequency: What You Need to Know

Three to five times a week is the sweet spot most creators land on. Daily works if you can sustain quality. Once a week is fine if your content is genuinely excellent.

Consistency beats frequency. Full stop.

Posting every day for two weeks and then disappearing for a month is one of the worst things you can do for growth. The algorithm notices patterns, and so do your followers.

---

## The Comment Section Is Your Secret Weapon

This one is massively underrated.

Leaving thoughtful comments on other people's posts, especially those with high engagement already, is one of the fastest ways to get seen by new audiences. Your comment shows up in your connections' feeds. It's essentially free reach.

Not "great post!" comments. Real ones. Add a perspective, share a relevant experience, ask a follow-up question. Ten minutes of genuine commenting a day can do more for your growth than an hour of agonising over your own posts.

This is sometimes called "community building" but that sounds a bit naff. Think of it as just being a decent contributor to the conversations already happening in your industry.

---

## The Types of Posts Growing Accounts Fastest Right Now

Look, I've watched a lot of accounts grow (and stall) over the past year. Here's what's working.

**Personal stories with a professional lesson.** Not therapy-session-level vulnerability, just honest reflections on what you've learned or got wrong. Australians especially respond well to this because we have a decent radar for anyone who sounds too polished or performative.

**Contrarian takes.** Pick something your industry widely believes and respectfully argue the other side. Even if not everyone agrees, you'll spark discussion.

**Behind-the-scenes content.** How you work, what your process looks like, what a weird Tuesday actually involved. People are curious.

**Data or observations from your own experience.** Not recycled stats from a Forbes article from 2023. Your actual numbers, your actual results.

## Building an Audience Beyond Your Network

Growing past your immediate circle requires being deliberate about a few things.

Hashtags still matter, just not as many. Two or three relevant ones per post is plenty. More than five and you start looking a bit desperate.

Tagging people, when it's genuinely relevant, puts your post in front of their audience too. Don't tag people randomly. That's annoying and everyone can tell.

Collaborating with other creators in your space is probably the most underused growth tactic on LinkedIn right now. A shared post, a conversation in the comments, a joint LinkedIn Live. It's the 2026 version of cross-promotion and it works a treat.

### Should You Bother With LinkedIn Newsletters?

Short answer: yes, if you can commit to it.

LinkedIn newsletters get a notification pushed to all your followers when you publish. That's genuinely valuable. And they tend to attract a more engaged, loyal reader than a standard post.

Monthly is achievable for most people. Don't promise weekly if you know you'll flake by week three.

## One Last Thing

Growing on LinkedIn in 2026 isn't some mysterious dark art. It's mostly about showing up consistently, saying interesting things, and actually engaging with other people like a human being.

The accounts growing fastest right now aren't the ones with the biggest budgets or the flashiest graphics. They're the ones being specific, being consistent, and being genuinely useful to the people they're trying to reach.

That's it, really.
